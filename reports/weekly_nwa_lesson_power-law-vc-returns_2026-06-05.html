The Power Law in VC Returns

June 5, 2026 · Topic: power-law-vc-returns

The power law is the single most important math in venture capital: a tiny minority of investments — empirically, the top ~10% — generate the great majority of returns (60–80% in AngelList and PitchBook data), while the rest produce modest outcomes or zero. This is not "Pareto" in the casual sense; it's a true heavy-tailed distribution where the outliers are not just larger than the median, they are categorically different. A single 100× winner pays for many years of losses, and the second-best company in your book is often less than 10% of the value of the best.

For angel and seed investors specifically, the power law is the reason portfolio construction is more important than picking. If outcomes are heavy-tailed, the only way to reliably land in the tail is to have enough swings — academic and practitioner consensus puts that floor at roughly 25–30 investments, with early-stage funds often deploying across 50–80 companies. The trap is the angel who writes 6 checks, picks well, and gets a fair-but-not-great outcome — statistically every individual angel deal has roughly the same chance of being the breakout, so under-diversifying isn't conviction, it's bad math. The conviction step happens before you say yes to a category; once you're in, you need enough lottery tickets in that category to capture its outliers.

The coaching insight a seasoned VC would share but business school won't: stop optimizing for your average outcome, start optimizing for your top-quartile outcome. Most angels track portfolio IRR like it's a public-market return — that's a category error. In a power-law game, the right metric is whether your single best deal is large enough to justify everything around it. That reframes diligence (you're not avoiding losers, you're hunting for the one company that could plausibly return your whole book), follow-on (you double down on the rare deal showing tail-shaped traction, even when it feels "expensive"), and emotional discipline (the writeoffs aren't failures — they're the cost of being in the game). This week's mega-rounds — Anthropic at $965B, Ramp at $44B, Generalist AI at $2B with Fei-Fei Li and Bezos Expeditions piling onto a 2-year-old robotics startup — are exactly what the tail looks like when it cashes. The seeds that produced those outcomes were planted in portfolios of 30+ bets, not 5.

Key Terms

Power Law Distribution — A statistical distribution in which a very small number of observations account for the majority of total value. In venture, ~10% of investments typically generate 60–80% of returns; the median return is often near zero.

Portfolio Construction — The deliberate sizing, pacing, and concentration of your investment book to match the statistical reality of the asset class. In VC, this means enough positions (≥25 for angels) at small enough check size to be diversified into the tail, and reserved follow-on capital to ride the winners.

Real-World Example

A16z's seed-and-Series-A investment in Anthropic is a textbook power-law outcome: a single position now anchoring a $965B private valuation, with the company filing a confidential S-1 this week. But the lesson is in the portfolio that made it possible. Andreessen Horowitz writes hundreds of seed and early-stage checks across its strategies; the ones that quietly returned 1× or zero are invisible from the outside, but they are what made Anthropic, Databricks, and the other tail-defining names economically feasible.

The opposite version: an angel who could only place 6 checks in 2020–22 and happened not to pick Anthropic is not a worse picker than Marc Andreessen — they just had structurally too few swings to participate in the category of tail outcomes that 2025–26 is now paying out.

Jamie's Action Prompt

This week, list your current angel book on one page, with check size, vintage, and a one-line current-traction note for each. Then answer two questions honestly: (1) how many active positions do you have, and how does that compare to the 25–30 minimum for a power-law-honest portfolio? (2) which 1–2 positions show tail-shaped signal (right team in a category that could be infrastructure)? Those are the names you should reserve follow-on capacity for, even if they don't feel "cheap" today.
