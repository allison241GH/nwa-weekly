SAFE Notes vs. Convertible Notes vs. Priced Rounds

May 9, 2026 · Topic: safe-notes-vs-convertible-notes-vs-priced-rounds

When a startup raises money, there are three primary instruments: a SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity), a convertible note, and a priced round. A SAFE is a one-page agreement where you give the company money now in exchange for the right to receive equity later (typically at the next priced round), with no interest and no maturity date. A convertible note is a loan — it accrues interest, has a maturity date, and converts to equity at the next qualifying round. A priced round is what it sounds like: a specific price per share is set today, and you buy shares at that price.

SAFEs (created by Y Combinator in 2013) have become the dominant instrument at pre-seed and seed because they're fast, cheap, and founder-friendly — no board seats, no interest, no legal fees above a few hundred dollars. Convertible notes were the standard before SAFEs and are still common, especially when investors want the psychological comfort of a "loan" and the leverage a maturity date creates. Priced rounds require a formal valuation, a term sheet, and legal work — typically $15K–$50K in legal fees — so they're usually reserved for Series A and beyond.

As an angel, you'll most often encounter SAFEs and convertible notes. The critical thing to understand: both instruments defer the valuation question. You're investing without knowing your price per share. That's fine — but only if you have a valuation cap that protects your upside. Without a cap, you could invest $50K at pre-seed and end up with the same price per share as someone who invests at Series A, when the risk was far lower. The cap sets a maximum valuation at which your investment converts, ensuring early risk is rewarded.

Here's what seasoned VCs know that first-time angels often miss: the instrument is less important than the terms. A SAFE with a $20M cap is worse for you than a convertible note with a $5M cap. Don't get distracted by the vehicle — focus on the cap, the discount rate (typically 15-25%), and whether it's a pre-money or post-money SAFE (YC switched to post-money SAFEs in 2018, which makes dilution math much cleaner for everyone). And never, ever invest on an uncapped SAFE — it means the founder either doesn't understand dilution or doesn't care about their early investors.

Key Terms

Valuation Cap — The maximum company valuation at which your SAFE or convertible note will convert to equity. If the Series A prices the company at $30M but your cap is $8M, your investment converts as if the company were worth $8M — giving you roughly 3.75x more shares per dollar than the Series A investors.

Discount Rate — A percentage reduction (typically 15-25%) on the Series A price per share that early investors receive as a reward for taking earlier risk. The discount and the cap both apply, and the investor gets whichever produces more shares.

Real-World Example

Look at Tessera Labs' $60M round led by a16z this week. At this stage, it's almost certainly a priced round — a16z doesn't do SAFEs at $60M. But Tessera's earliest investors? They likely came in on SAFEs at a fraction of this valuation. If an angel invested $50K on a SAFE with a $10M cap, and Tessera's Series A priced at $200M post-money, that angel's shares would be worth roughly $1M at paper value — a 20x return from a one-page document. That's the power of early-stage instruments with the right cap.

Jamie's Action Prompt

Pull up the last SAFE or convertible note you reviewed (or look at a YC post-money SAFE template). Check three things: (1) Is there a valuation cap? (2) Is it pre-money or post-money? (3) What discount rate, if any? If you can answer those three questions for any deal, you understand 80% of what matters at the seed stage.
