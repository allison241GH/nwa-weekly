Term Sheet Anatomy — Key Terms and What to Negotiate

May 5, 2026 · Topic: term-sheet-anatomy

A term sheet is the handshake before the contract — a non-binding document that outlines the key economic and governance terms of an investment. It's typically 3-8 pages, and while most of it is "standard," the terms that aren't standard are where deals get made or broken. As an angel, your ability to read a term sheet fluently — and know which terms actually matter — separates you from the crowd that just writes checks and hopes.

The terms fall into two buckets: economic terms (how the money works) and control terms (who decides what). Economic terms include valuation (pre-money vs. post-money), liquidation preference (1x non-participating is founder-friendly; anything higher or participating preferred tilts toward investors), option pool size and whether it's pre- or post-money (a pre-money pool effectively lowers the founder's valuation — a common negotiation trap), and anti-dilution provisions (full ratchet vs. weighted average). Control terms include board composition, protective provisions (veto rights on key decisions like future fundraising, M&A, or changes to the charter), information rights, and drag-along provisions.

Here's the coaching insight most first-time angels miss: don't fight every term — fight the right ones. Liquidation preference and anti-dilution are the terms that determine whether you actually make money in a downside or moderate exit scenario. Board seats and protective provisions determine whether you have a voice when things go sideways. Pro-rata rights determine whether you can maintain your ownership in winners. Everything else is largely noise. A seasoned VC will tell you: "I've never lost money because of a bad information rights clause. I've lost millions because I didn't negotiate liquidation preference."

Key Terms

Liquidation Preference — The order and multiple at which investors get paid back before common shareholders (founders, employees) in an exit. A "1x non-participating" preference means investors get their money back OR convert to common and share pro rata — whichever is higher. "Participating preferred" means investors get their money back AND share in the remaining proceeds, which can dramatically reduce founder payouts in modest exits.

Option Pool Shuffle — When a term sheet specifies a 15-20% option pool created from pre-money valuation, it effectively reduces the price per share founders receive. A $10M pre-money with a 20% option pool means the founder's effective pre-money is $8M. Always check whether the pool is carved from pre-money or post-money — it changes the math significantly.

Real-World Example

Look at Sierra's $950M Series E announced this week. At $15.8B post-money, the term sheet almost certainly includes heavy protective provisions — at this stage, Tiger Global and GV would negotiate for anti-dilution protection, board observer seats, and liquidation preferences that protect their massive check. But notice: Bret Taylor (former Salesforce co-CEO, current OpenAI chair) has enough leverage to likely negotiate 1x non-participating and maintain founder-friendly governance. Founder leverage scales with traction — Sierra's $150M ARR in 8 quarters gives Taylor negotiating power that a pre-revenue founder simply doesn't have.

Jamie's Action Prompt

At today's NWA Spring Summit, when you see a new venture presentation, mentally map the likely term sheet. Ask yourself: what liquidation preference would I want? What protective provisions would I need? How large is the option pool and is it pre- or post-money? Practice reading the economics behind the pitch — it will sharpen your eye for the deals you want to be in.
