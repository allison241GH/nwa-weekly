Board Seats, Observer Rights, and Governance

July 11, 2026 · Topic: board-seats-observer-rights-governance

A board seat is not a trophy — it is a job with legal teeth. When you take a seat on a startup's board of directors, you owe fiduciary duties to the company and all its shareholders, not to the fund or the check that put you there. That distinction trips up first-time angels constantly: the instinct is to represent "your" money, but the law says a director must act in the company's best interest even when that conflicts with your position. A board seat gives you a formal vote on hiring the CEO, approving budgets and financings, and blessing an eventual sale — real control, and real liability. An observer seat, by contrast, gets you into the room and the data flow but with no vote and no fiduciary duty — you can watch, advise, and stay informed without the legal exposure. For most early-stage angels writing sub-institutional checks, an observer right is the better deal: you get the information and the relationship without the conflict-of-interest minefield and the D&O liability.

Why this matters at seed and Series A specifically: board composition is the governance structure of a company that has no other real governance. There is no audit committee, no independent chair, often no HR — the three-to-five people around that table decide everything that matters. As an investor, the terms you negotiate here determine whether you can actually influence the company or merely watch it. Protective provisions — the list of major actions (selling the company, raising a down round, taking on debt above a threshold, changing the option pool) that require preferred-shareholder or specific-director approval — are how a minority investor keeps a veto on the decisions that can wipe them out, without needing majority control. A clean seed governance package is usually one investor board seat (for the lead), observer rights for meaningful non-lead investors, and a tight set of protective provisions. Bloated boards — five investor seats at seed — are a red flag that founders gave away control too early.

Here is the coaching insight business school won't tell you: the best board members are recruited out of the room as fast as they're recruited in. A seasoned VC treats a board seat as a term that should shrink over time — early investors ideally roll off (or convert to observers) as the company matures and adds independent directors and later-stage leads who bring more relevant scar tissue. Clinging to a seat past your usefulness is how you become the director who slows a company down. And the single most valuable thing a great board member does is not in any meeting: it's the calibrated, private call to the CEO between meetings. Governance is a relationship, not a quarterly ritual — the seat is just the excuse to have earned the CEO's trust. If you take a seat, over-index on being the director the CEO calls first with bad news, not the one they manage around.

Key Terms

Board Observer — A contractual right to attend board meetings and receive board materials without a vote or a formal fiduciary duty. It gives an investor information and a voice while avoiding the legal liability and conflict exposure of a full directorship — usually the right fit for non-lead angel investors.

Protective Provisions — A negotiated list of major corporate actions (e.g., selling the company, issuing senior securities, raising debt above a cap, altering the board size) that cannot be taken without the approval of the preferred shareholders or a designated director. They let a minority investor hold a veto on company-altering decisions without owning a majority of the votes.

Real-World Example

When Sequoia and other early investors backed companies through their explosive growth, a recurring pattern held: the founder kept board control at seed and Series A, giving investors one seat plus protective provisions rather than a controlling bloc — and the healthiest outcomes came where investors added independent directors as the company scaled rather than stacking more investor seats. The cautionary flip side is WeWork, where a weak, founder-dominated board with insufficient independent oversight failed to check governance and spending problems until a failed IPO forced the issue.

Same lesson from both directions: at the early stage, who sits on the board and what they can veto matters far more than how many seats any one investor holds. This week's news that new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh recruited a16z's Marc Andreessen onto an external policy task force is a useful reminder even at the macro level — who you put in the governance room shapes the decisions that come out of it.

Jamie's Action Prompt

Pick one company in (or near) the NWA pipeline where you're considering a check. Before you invest, write down the exact governance ask you'd make: full board seat, observer right, or neither — and why, given your check size and how much value you can genuinely add between meetings. Then draft the two or three protective provisions you'd actually care about. If your honest answer is "observer, plus a veto on down rounds and a sale," you've just done the diligence most first-time angels skip entirely.
