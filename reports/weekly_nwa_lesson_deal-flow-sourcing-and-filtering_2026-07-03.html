Deal Flow: Sourcing and Filtering Opportunities

July 3, 2026 · Topic: deal-flow-sourcing-and-filtering

Deal flow is the top of your funnel: the stream of investable companies that reach your desk before you decide which get a meeting, which get diligence, and which get a check. Every angel obsesses over picking winners, but returns are set at least as much by what you get to pick from. If your deal flow is the same TechCrunch-famous rounds everyone else sees, you're competing on price and access with people who have more of both. The entire game at the top of the funnel is building a flow that is (a) high in volume, (b) genuinely differentiated from what other investors see, and (c) filterable fast — because the scarce resource isn't capital, it's your attention.

For angel and seed investors specifically, this matters more than at any later stage, because early-stage signal is thin and the noise is overwhelming. You will see hundreds of decks a year and can seriously diligence maybe a dozen. That ratio means your filter is your real edge: a crisp, written thesis and a small set of disqualifying gates let you say no in ninety seconds and reserve your energy for the handful worth a deep look. Sourcing without filtering just drowns you; filtering without sourcing starves you. You need both, tuned together — and the flight-to-quality market of 2026, where seed volume is down ~29% but median pre-money is up ~19%, punishes anyone whose only flow is the overpriced, over-syndicated names everyone is bidding up.

Here's what a seasoned VC would tell a first-time angel that business school won't: proprietary deal flow is manufactured, not found. It comes from being known for something — a niche, a thesis, a reputation for being genuinely helpful to founders before you invest. Write publicly about a specific corner of the market, help two founders with no expectation of a deal, run a useful gathering, and within a year the best companies in that niche route to you first, before the round is competitive. The counterintuitive part: a narrow, opinionated thesis increases your flow, because founders self-select toward the investor who obviously gets their space. Generalists get the leftovers; specialists get the first call.

Key Terms

Proprietary Deal Flow — Investment opportunities that come to you through relationships, reputation, or expertise before they become widely competitive — as opposed to "intermediated" flow (banker emails, demo-day scrums) that everyone sees at once. Proprietary flow is where angels win on access and price.

Anti-Portfolio — The list of great companies you saw and passed on (Bessemer famously publishes theirs). Tracking your anti-portfolio turns misses into a feedback loop: it exposes the blind spots in your filter so you stop making the same "too early / too weird / not my space" mistake twice.

Real-World Example

Y Combinator is the industrialized version of this. YC doesn't find the best founders one by one — it built a brand so strong that the best founders apply to it, manufacturing proprietary flow at scale, then filters ruthlessly (a low-single-digit acceptance rate) so investors at Demo Day are pre-screened. Notice the structure: powerful sourcing engine plus a brutal filter. Angels can borrow the logic without the scale — a sharp public thesis is your "brand that attracts applicants," and a written set of gates is your "acceptance rate."

This week's Together AI ($800M) and Joulent ($1.75B) rounds are the opposite end: by the time a company is raising nine figures at a multi-billion valuation, deal flow is fully intermediated and priced — the angel edge was two or three rounds earlier, when knowing the founder or the niche was worth more than knowing the round was open.

Jamie's Action Prompt

Write your one-paragraph deal-flow thesis this week — the specific slice of the market (e.g., "vertical AI applications for regulated industries") where NWA should be the first call, not the fifth. Then define three disqualifying gates that let you pass in under two minutes. Test it against the last ten deals you saw: how many would your gates have correctly screened in or out — and where did your filter fail?
