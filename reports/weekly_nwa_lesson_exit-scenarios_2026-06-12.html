Exit Scenarios: M&A, IPO, Secondary Sales

June 12, 2026 · Topic: exit-scenarios

An exit is the only event where an angel investment turns from a number on a cap table into money in your account. There are really three doors: an acquisition (someone buys the company — by far the most common outcome), an IPO (the company sells shares to the public, the rarest and most celebrated), and a secondary sale (you sell your shares to another private buyer before any company-wide event). This week handed you a live example of each pressure: SpaceX priced the largest IPO in history and popped 19%, NinjaOne let early investors and employees cash out ~$400M in a pure secondary at a $12.3B mark, and Anthropic's confidential S-1 sits in the IPO queue behind them.

For an angel or seed investor specifically, the practical stakes are about time and control — and you have almost none of either. M&A and IPO are decisions made by the founder, the board, and the later, bigger investors; as a small early holder you are along for the ride. That is exactly why secondaries matter so much more to you than they do to a fund: a secondary is the one exit you can sometimes initiate yourself, and in a market where companies stay private for 10+ years, it may be your only realistic path to liquidity before you're 80. The mistake first-time angels make is treating "exit" as a single far-off IPO fantasy instead of a menu of probability-weighted paths, most of which are acquisitions at modest multiples and a few of which are secondaries you have to actively go find.

Here's what a seasoned VC will tell you that business school won't: the exit is priced into the entry, whether you noticed or not. When you take a SAFE with a high cap on a company whose only believable exit is a $50M acqui-hire, the math is already dead — you just won't feel it for six years. Conversely, the reason the smartest angels obsess over "could this be a category-defining company" is not romance; it's that only IPO-scale or large strategic-M&A outcomes return a power-law portfolio, and everything else is a rounding error. And watch the liquidation preference stack above you: in a soft acquisition, preferred investors with 1x (or worse, participating) preferences get paid first, and common/early money can walk away with nothing from a sale that looked like a "win" in the headline.

Key Terms

Secondary Sale — The sale of existing shares from one investor (or employee) to another buyer, rather than the company issuing new shares. It gives early holders liquidity without an IPO or acquisition. NinjaOne's $400M raise this week was largely secondary: the company didn't pocket most of it; early investors and employees did.

Liquidation Preference — A term that dictates who gets paid first, and how much, when a company is sold or wound down. A "1x non-participating" preference means preferred holders get their money back before common shareholders see a dollar; "participating" means they get their money back and share in the rest. In a down-side or modest exit, a heavy preference stack can leave early common/angel money with little or nothing.

Real-World Example

Contrast two doors from this week. SpaceX is the IPO fantasy made real — a company that stayed private long enough to compound into a >$2T debut, rewarding the earliest believers enormously but only after ~24 years and many intervening secondary rounds that let early employees and angels take partial liquidity along the way. NinjaOne is the quieter, more instructive case for an angel: no IPO, no acquisition, just a $12.3B secondary that turned paper into cash for early backers and staff while the company kept running.

Most of your realized returns, if you do this well, will look more like NinjaOne's mechanism than SpaceX's headline — partial, negotiated, and initiated well before any bell rings.

Jamie's Action Prompt

Pull up two or three of your live angel positions and, for each, write down the single most probable exit door (acquisition, IPO, or secondary) and the rough valuation at which it pays you back — then check whether you actually know the liquidation-preference stack sitting above your shares. If you don't know it for even one position, that's this week's email to the founder or lead investor. Knowing your exit math before the exit is the difference between investing and gambling.
