Convertible Note Mechanics — Discount, Cap, Interest

May 1, 2026 · Topic: convertible-note-mechanics

A convertible note is a short-term debt instrument that converts into equity at a future priced round — typically a Series A. Instead of negotiating a valuation today (which is hard and contentious at pre-seed/seed), the founder borrows money with the understanding that the note will convert into shares when a larger, more sophisticated investor sets the price. It's a handshake that says: "We'll figure out what I'm worth later, and you'll get a deal for believing in me early."

Three mechanics control how that "deal" works. The discount (typically 15–25%) gives you shares at a lower price than Series A investors pay — your reward for taking early risk. The valuation cap sets a ceiling on the price at which your note converts, protecting you if the company's valuation skyrockets before the priced round. The interest rate (usually 4–8% simple interest) accrues on the principal and converts into additional shares — it's modest, but it's real equity over a 12–24 month note term.

Here's what a seasoned VC would tell you that business school won't: the cap matters more than the discount, and most first-time angels negotiate the wrong one. If a startup's cap is $8M and they raise their Series A at $40M, your shares convert at $8M — an effective 80% discount. The stated 20% discount only matters if the Series A valuation is lower than the cap. Always negotiate the cap first. And watch out for notes without caps — they're called "uncapped notes," and they're a gift to the founder at your expense. The only time an uncapped note makes sense is if you're getting a very steep discount (30%+) and you have high conviction the next round won't be at a crazy valuation.

Key Terms

Valuation Cap — The maximum company valuation at which a convertible note will convert to equity. If the next round's valuation exceeds the cap, the note converts at the cap — giving the early investor more shares per dollar than the new investors.

Discount Rate — The percentage reduction from the Series A share price that a convertible note holder receives at conversion. A 20% discount means you pay $0.80 for every $1.00 share the Series A investors buy.

Real-World Example

When Dropbox raised its seed round in 2007, Y Combinator and Sequoia invested via convertible notes. The notes had a reasonable cap that, when Dropbox raised its Series A at a $25M valuation from Accel Partners, converted at a fraction of that price — giving the seed investors roughly 4x the equity per dollar compared to Series A participants. Drew Houston later said the convertible note structure let him avoid a painful valuation negotiation that would have slowed the company at its most fragile stage. This is exactly how the instrument is supposed to work: speed for the founder, protection for the early investor.

Jamie's Action Prompt

Pull up the last NWA deal that used a convertible note. Check three things: (1) What was the cap relative to the company's traction at the time? (2) Was there both a cap AND a discount, or just one? (3) Has the note converted yet, and if so, what was the effective price you paid vs. the Series A price? Understanding the math on a real deal you've done is worth 10 hours of theory.
