Anthropic held its first European developer event in London this week, announcing the "dreaming" feature: Claude Code agents now write memory notes to themselves mid-task that other agents can read to coordinate, learn from prior errors, and accelerate onboarding to a codebase. Anthropic also disclosed that Claude performs at the level of a mid-level software engineer on coding tasks.

Why it matters to Jamie: "Dreaming" is the first native multi-agent memory primitive in Claude Code — it transforms agent sessions from stateless sprints into cumulative institutional knowledge. For an Applied AI Leader building agentic workflows for NWAI or teaching AI orchestration at FAU, this is the architectural shift that makes persistent agent teams viable.

Suggested action: Test the dreaming feature on a bounded NWAI task — it directly addresses the cold-start problem in long-running agent workflows.

Section C — Trends to Watch

AI Cognitive Debt: Are Your Employees Getting Dumber?

A BCG follow-up study of 244 consultants identified three AI collaboration modes: Cyborgs (60%) engage iteratively and build expertise; Centaurs (14%) use AI selectively and deepen domain knowledge; Self-Automators (27%) delegate entire workflows and develop neither AI skills nor domain expertise. Researchers call the self-automator pattern "cognitive debt" — performance holds short-term while underlying capacity quietly degrades. The crucial distinction: AI offloads reasoning itself, not just computation or navigation.

Why it matters to Jamie: This framework is directly teachable at FAU Executive Education — executives need to consciously choose the Cyborg mode, not default into Self-Automation. The BCG model gives you a crisp diagnostic for an entire AI for Business Leaders session.

AI Infrastructure Capital Is Clustering — The Deployment Gap Bet

$5.5B in VC capital deployed this quarter specifically targeting what investors call the "deployment gap" — the distance between AI capability and production-grade enterprise deployment. Modal ($355M), Rhoda AI ($450M Series A launch), and multiple infrastructure-focused rounds confirm this is an emerging consensus: the compute, sandbox, and inference layer is the durable value creation layer, not the application layer. The AWS vs. dot-com analogy is now being used openly by top-tier investors.

Why it matters to Jamie: At the seed/angel stage, understanding where the infrastructure premium is pricing in helps you evaluate application-layer deals more honestly — what infrastructure does this startup depend on, and is that dependency a moat or a commodity cost?

The Series A Bar Has Moved — Seed Companies Need New Math

2026 Series A investors now require $2M+ ARR and $0.50 burn per $1 of new ARR — significantly higher than 2023–2024 benchmarks. The practical implication: companies raising seed rounds today need more runway and a higher growth bar to reach their A than founders are currently budgeting for.

Why it matters to Jamie: As an angel setting valuations and terms at seed stage, the A-round milestone is the exit ramp your portfolio companies need to reach. If the A bar has moved, your seed company survival runway math needs to be stress-tested — and your due diligence should probe burn efficiency harder than two years ago.
