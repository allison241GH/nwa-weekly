Pre-money vs. Post-money Valuation

May 31, 2026 · Topic: pre-money-vs-post-money-valuation

Every term sheet negotiation pivots on two numbers that look similar but mean entirely different things. Pre-money valuation is what the company is worth before new investment comes in. Post-money valuation is what it's worth after — simply pre-money plus the round size. The distinction sounds trivial until you realize that ownership percentages, dilution calculations, and every downstream pro-rata right all hinge on which number you're using. An investor putting $5M into a company at a $20M pre-money valuation owns 20% (5M ÷ 25M post-money). Put that same $5M in at a $20M post-money valuation and they own 25% — a structurally different deal, even though the headline number looks identical. Founders and angels who blur this distinction in negotiations routinely give away more ownership than they intend.

For angel and seed investors, this matters in two directions. First, when you're writing a check: always confirm whether the valuation a founder quotes is pre or post — and if they don't know, that's a yellow flag on financial sophistication. Second, when you're interpreting a company's fundraising history in your due diligence: a startup that raised $3M on a $12M post-money cap last year is a materially different situation than one that raised $3M on a $12M pre-money — the latter is already significantly more diluted. This week's Anthropic raise illustrates this at scale: Anthropic's $65B round at a $965B post-money valuation means the $65B is included in that headline figure. The pre-money was approximately $900B — a distinction that matters enormously when calculating investor ownership stakes and the implied return multiple for earlier shareholders.

The coaching insight seasoned VCs give first-time angels that business school skips: negotiate on pre-money, not post-money. When a founder says "we're raising at a $10M valuation," always ask "pre or post?" — and always model both scenarios before signing. SAFEs with post-money caps (now the YC standard) automatically include your investment in the cap calculation, which means you own less than a pre-money cap SAFE of the same dollar amount. Most angels who've been burned by dilution surprises at priced rounds can trace it back to this single confusion. Know the arithmetic cold before you negotiate; never let the ambiguity slide.

Key Terms

Pre-money valuation — The agreed-upon value of a company before a new round of investment is added. Used as the baseline for calculating how much ownership an investor receives for their check size. Formula: Investor ownership % = Investment ÷ (Pre-money valuation + Investment).

Post-money valuation — The value of the company after the new investment is included. Simply: Pre-money valuation + round size. This is the number most commonly cited in press coverage of funding rounds. On a YC post-money SAFE, the cap is post-money — meaning your investment is already inside the dilution math.

Real-World Example

When Anthropic announced its $65B Series H this week at a "$965B valuation," that figure is post-money — the $65B is folded into the headline number. Sequoia, Coatue, and the other new investors negotiated their ownership percentages against an implied pre-money of approximately $900B. Early investors (including Google and Spark Capital) see their stakes diluted by the new entrants' ownership, calculated from the post-money cap. At the seed stage, the math is the same — just with more zeros removed. A founder raising a $500K SAFE on a $4M post-money cap is offering early investors approximately 12.5% ownership — not 12.5% of a $4M pre-money company plus the $500K. Getting comfortable with the arithmetic at the small end makes the large-end headlines instantly readable.

Jamie's Action Prompt

Review the last two deals in your NWA pipeline that quoted a valuation without explicitly specifying pre or post-money. Recalculate the ownership percentage under both interpretations, note the delta, and bring the clarifying question — "Is that pre or post?" — as a standard opening in your next diligence call. The founders who answer immediately and correctly are demonstrating the financial fluency you want in a cap table partner.
