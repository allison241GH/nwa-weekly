## Section G — Learning Venture Investing

### This Week's Lesson: Angel Syndicates and SPVs

An **angel syndicate** is a group of individual investors who pool capital to invest in a single startup deal, typically organized by a "lead" who sources the deal, negotiates terms, and manages the investment. A **Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV)** is the legal entity — usually an LLC — created to hold that pooled investment. Think of the syndicate as the team; the SPV is the jersey they wear for one specific game.

For angel investors, syndicates solve three critical problems at once. First, they let you write smaller checks into deals you couldn't access alone — a $25K commitment through a syndicate can get you into a round that has a $250K minimum. Second, they give you access to deal flow curated by experienced leads who have track records and founder relationships you may not have yet. Third, they provide built-in due diligence — a good syndicate lead has already vetted the company, and the collective intelligence of co-investors adds another layer of scrutiny.

Here's what the textbooks don't tell you: **the economics of syndicates aren't always founder-friendly.** A syndicate lead typically charges 20% carry (share of profits) and sometimes a management fee on top. When a founder sees "$500K from a syndicate," they're actually dealing with one decision-maker (the lead) plus dozens of passive investors who may never attend a board meeting, make introductions, or provide strategic value. Smart founders increasingly ask: "Is this syndicate capital or smart capital?" As an angel, your job is to make sure you're adding value beyond the check — especially when competing with retail vehicles like Robinhood's RVII that offer passive exposure without the carry.

#### Key Terms

- **Carry (Carried Interest)** — The share of investment profits paid to the syndicate lead or fund manager, typically 20%. If a $100K SPV investment returns $500K, the lead takes 20% of the $400K profit ($80K), and investors split the remaining $320K pro-rata. Carry only applies to profits — if the investment loses money, the lead earns nothing.
- **SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle)** — A single-purpose LLC formed to make one investment. It appears as a single line item on the startup's cap table (cleaner for founders) but represents multiple individual investors underneath. SPVs typically dissolve when the investment is exited.

#### Real-World Example

AngelList pioneered the modern syndicate model. When Naval Ravikant led a syndicate into Uber's early rounds, hundreds of angels got exposure to a generational company through a single SPV. The SPV showed up as one entity on Uber's cap table, keeping it clean for the company while giving each syndicate member pro-rata ownership. The lesson: syndicates work best when the lead has genuine access and conviction, not just a distribution list.

#### Jamie's Action Prompt

Look at your last three NWA deals. For each one, ask: "Would this deal have been better structured as a syndicate with a lead angel, or as a direct investment?" What changed about the founder relationship, the due diligence depth, and your post-investment influence?
