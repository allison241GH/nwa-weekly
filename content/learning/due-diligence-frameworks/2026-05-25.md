Seed-stage due diligence is not the same exercise as Series B diligence. When you're writing a $25K–$100K check into a three-person team with $50K in revenue, you cannot run a KPMG-style audit and emerge with a useful answer in time to matter. Seed-stage DD is fundamentally about probabilistic pattern recognition: are these the right people, in the right market, at the right moment, with the right entry price? The diligence framework has to match the stage. Over-engineer it and your deal flow speed dies; under-engineer it and you build a portfolio of elegant-sounding disasters. The practical skill is a repeatable checklist you can run in 1–2 weeks that surfaces the 3–5 signals most predictive of early success — and that stops you from performing "deal theater" (diligence that feels rigorous but doesn't actually reduce risk).

The core of any early-stage DD framework has four pillars: **founder quality**, **market timing**, **product insight**, and **entry terms**. Founder quality dominates at pre-seed/seed because everything else is still hypothesis. You're betting on execution under conditions of maximum uncertainty. Market timing is the second pillar — a great founder in a market that isn't ready yet produces a lesson, not a return. Product insight (does the team have proprietary understanding of why customers will change behavior?) separates genuine product thinkers from slide-deck founders. Entry terms determine whether you make money even if the company modestly succeeds — valuation and structure matter more than most angels admit when they're excited about a founder.

The coaching insight that most business school curricula skip: **the back-channel reference check is the most valuable hour in your entire diligence process.** Great references provided by the founder will always be great — they were chosen for that reason. The value is in the reference call with someone who has worked with the founders but wasn't on their list: a former colleague, a co-founder from a prior venture, or a previous investor who didn't participate in this round. That call is where you learn whether the founder listens or just performs listening, how they handle adversity, and whether they treat people well under pressure. Marc Andreessen has said his biggest losers were situations where a founder misrepresented their track record. The fix is always the same: talk to people who have no incentive to sell you.

## Key Terms

**Reference check** — A direct conversation with someone who has worked with or evaluated the founders, conducted independently of the formal pitch process. The most valuable reference checks are back-channel calls — people not on the founder's reference list — where the caller has no incentive to sell you on the company.

**Deal thesis** — A 1–2 sentence statement of specifically why *this* company can win in *this* market *now*, written in a form that is falsifiable: you can identify what evidence would prove it wrong. A deal thesis that can't be falsified isn't a thesis — it's a hope.

## Real-World Example

When Sequoia evaluated Stripe in 2011, the DD focused not on revenue (there was none) or product completeness (the API was barely functional) but on a single founder insight: Patrick Collison understood that developers were the real buyer of payments infrastructure — not CTOs, not finance teams — and that no one was building for them. Sequoia's diligence question was essentially: "Is this insight real, and are these the founders who can turn it into a product developers will evangelize?" They validated it by watching early developer response in beta. The deal thesis was testable: if developers adopted Stripe without a sales team, the insight was real. They did. Sequoia's $600K seed check returned over 1,000x. The lesson: your diligence questions have to probe whether the founders have a proprietary insight that only comes from lived experience — not from market research that any analyst can run.

This week's Cluster 3 news is relevant here: Mercury's $200M Series D at $5.2B valuation is partly a story about Sequoia, a16z, and Coatue's follow-on investing discipline. They identified the Mercury thesis early (one in three US startups, a product-led growth model that doesn't require a sales team), validated it over four years of profitability, and kept their pro-rata rights. That's what patient, conviction-based follow-on investing looks like — and it starts with rigorous seed-stage diligence.

## Jamie's Action Prompt

Before the Captain Compliance due diligence meeting on June 1, write a one-paragraph deal thesis in this format: *"Captain Compliance can win [specific market] because [proprietary insight the founders have that competitors don't], and I will know this is true if [specific, observable evidence from the demo].* Then identify the three specific things the product demo must show you that would either confirm or break that thesis. If you can't write the thesis in one paragraph before the meeting, you haven't done enough pre-work — and you'll be doing deal theater instead of diligence.
