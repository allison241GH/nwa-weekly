Every angel deal starts with the same question: how does my money go in, and what do I get back? The three primary instruments are SAFEs (Simple Agreement for Future Equity), convertible notes, and priced equity rounds. Each has a different risk profile, legal cost, and set of negotiation levers — and choosing wrong can cost you dilution, control, or time.

A **SAFE** is not debt. It's a contract that says "I give you money now, and I get equity later when you raise a priced round." There's no interest rate, no maturity date, and no repayment obligation. Y Combinator invented the SAFE in 2013, and it now dominates pre-seed and seed deals — roughly 90% of pre-seed rounds in 2025 used SAFEs. They're fast (often a 5-page document), cheap ($0-2K in legal fees), and founder-friendly. The key terms are the **valuation cap** (the max valuation at which your SAFE converts) and the **discount** (typically 15-25% off the next round's price).

A **convertible note** is debt that converts to equity. It carries interest (typically 4-8%), a maturity date (usually 18-24 months), and all the legal complexity of a loan agreement. Notes give investors more protection: if the startup doesn't raise again before maturity, you have a legal claim. But that protection comes with friction — $2-5K in legal costs, more negotiation, and a maturity-date time bomb that can force awkward conversations. Convertible notes were the standard before SAFEs and are still common in markets outside Silicon Valley or when investors want more downside protection.

Here's what a seasoned VC would tell a first-time angel: **SAFEs are faster and cheaper, but they can stack up dangerously.** Because SAFEs have no maturity date, founders can raise multiple SAFE rounds without ever triggering conversion — meaning your ownership percentage is unknown until a priced round happens. Always ask: "How many SAFEs are outstanding, and at what caps?" If a founder has stacked $3M in SAFEs across five different caps, the cap table is a minefield. The simplicity of the instrument doesn't mean the situation is simple.

## Key Terms

**Valuation Cap** — The maximum company valuation at which your investment converts to equity. If you invest on a $10M cap and the Series A prices at $30M, your shares convert at the $10M price — giving you 3x the equity per dollar compared to Series A investors. The cap is the single most important term in a SAFE or convertible note.

**Discount Rate** — A percentage reduction on the price-per-share in the next round. A 20% discount on a $5/share Series A means you convert at $4/share. If a SAFE has both a cap and a discount, the investor gets whichever produces more shares (the better deal).

## Real-World Example

When Airbnb raised its seed round in 2009 from Sequoia Capital, it used a convertible note — SAFEs didn't exist yet. The note converted at Airbnb's Series A valuation, and Sequoia's early bet turned into one of the most valuable seed investments in history. Today, the same deal would almost certainly be done on a SAFE with a valuation cap, saving weeks of legal negotiation and thousands in fees. YC-backed companies now close their entire seed round on a standard post-money SAFE in as little as 48 hours from handshake to wire.
