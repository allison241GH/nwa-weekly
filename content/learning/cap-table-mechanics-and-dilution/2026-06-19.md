A cap table is just a ledger of who owns what — but the word that actually matters is *fully-diluted*. Your ownership isn't the number of shares you hold divided by shares issued today; it's your shares divided by every share that *could* exist once all options, warrants, SAFEs, and convertible notes convert. A founder who proudly says "you own 5%" off the issued count may be quietly handing you 4% on a fully-diluted basis, and the gap only widens at the next round. Dilution is the mechanical consequence of issuing new shares: when the company sells 20% of itself in a Series A, everyone who came before — founders, angels, the option pool — gets their slice of the pie reduced proportionally, even though the *number* of shares you own never changed.

For an angel writing early checks, this is the single most under-appreciated math in the game, because you take the *most* dilution of anyone. You buy at the highest risk and the lowest price, then get diluted through every subsequent round before any liquidity event — a seed position can easily be cut in half by the time a company reaches Series C. The practical stakes are brutal and simple: a great company can still be a mediocre *investment* for you if you fail to model how thin your ownership gets by exit, and whether your check even matters at the exit valuation that's realistic (this is where the [[power-law-vc-returns]] math and [[pre-money-vs-post-money-valuation]] mechanics collide).

Here's the coaching insight business school skips: **dilution isn't inherently bad — un-priced dilution is.** A round that dilutes you 20% but doubles the company's value is a win; you own less of much more. The thing a seasoned VC watches isn't the dilution percentage, it's *what got bought with it.* And the trap to learn cold is the **option pool shuffle**: founders and later investors routinely expand the employee option pool *before* a new round and carve it out of the *pre-money* valuation — which means the dilution from that pool falls entirely on existing shareholders (you), not the incoming investor. It looks like a neutral housekeeping line on the term sheet. It is not. It is a price cut aimed squarely at the people already on the cap table.

## Key Terms

**Fully-Diluted Shares** — The total share count assuming every option, warrant, SAFE, and convertible note has been exercised or converted. Always compute your ownership against this number, never against issued shares — the difference is your real stake.

**Option Pool Shuffle** — The practice of creating or expanding the employee option pool out of the pre-money valuation just before a financing, so that existing shareholders (not the new investor) absorb the dilution. A larger pre-money pool effectively lowers the true price the new investor pays.

## Real-World Example

This week's seed market made the point for us: multiple AI startups priced **$50–66M seed rounds at $300M+ valuations** (NewCore raised $66M at a reported $300M cap), and YC's S26 batch standardized a **$500K-in-USDC** package across ~196 companies. Picture an angel who put $100K into one of those companies on a SAFE at a $15M cap two years earlier. On paper they "own" ~0.67%. But after a $300M seed, an expanded option pool carved from pre-money, and a SAFE stack that all converts at once, that position can quietly land closer to ~0.4% fully-diluted — before the company has even raised a true Series A. The headline valuation went up; the angel's *slice* went down. Whether that's a good or bad outcome depends entirely on one question they should have modeled at entry: does 0.4% of this company's realistic exit clear my power-law hurdle?

## Jamie's Action Prompt

Take one company in NWA's active pipeline and build a two-minute dilution model: start from your entry ownership on a fully-diluted basis, then haircut it through two hypothetical future rounds (assume ~20% dilution each) and ask what your stake is worth at three exit valuations — $100M, $500M, $2B. If your position doesn't return the fund at the *middle* scenario, the deal needs either a lower entry price, pro-rata rights to defend your stake, or a pass. Then, on the next term sheet you see, find the option-pool line and check whether it's carved from pre- or post-money — and renegotiate it if it's pre.
