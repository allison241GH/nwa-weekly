At pre-seed and seed, you are not underwriting a business — you're underwriting a person, because the business in front of you will change. The deck describes a product that probably won't survive contact with the market; what *will* persist is the founder's judgment when the plan breaks. So the central question of early-stage evaluation isn't "is this a good idea?" — it's "is this the person who will figure out the right idea faster than their money runs out?" That reframe is the whole game. Ideas are cheap and convergent (this week alone, three different teams were funded to build "world models"); the scarce asset is a founder with unusual *insight* into a specific market and the *velocity* to act on it.

For an angel, founder evaluation matters more than it does for a late-stage fund, and not less — you have the least data and the longest time horizon, so the founder is almost the entire signal. The trap is that founders are, by selection, exceptional storytellers; the pitch is the one thing they've optimized hardest. Your job is to evaluate the things they *haven't* staged: how they think when you push back, how they talk about their co-founder, what they actually did (not planned) in the last 90 days, and whether their explanation of *why this market, why them, why now* is earned or borrowed. Look for **founder-market fit** — a non-obvious, hard-won reason this specific person is unreasonably suited to this specific problem — and for evidence of *speed*: shipped things, recruited above their weight, got to customers without permission.

Here's the coaching insight business school won't give you: **the reference checks you don't ask for are the ones that matter.** Anyone can give you three glowing references — they're curated. The real signal comes from the *backchannel*: the former boss who wasn't on the list, the engineer who quit, the early customer who churned. A seasoned investor spends more time triangulating a founder's reputation among people who *aren't* incentivized to flatter them than parsing the financial model. And be ruthlessly honest about the difference between *charisma* and *substance* — this week's market is paying unicorn prices for pedigree alone (a16z seeding an ex-Google/Anthropic/xAI team at Mirendil; a $2.3B Series A for General Intuition). Pedigree is a real signal of *access and capability*, but it is not the same as founder-market fit or integrity, and conflating the two is how smart money overpays.

## Key Terms

**Founder-Market Fit** — The degree to which a founder's specific background, insight, and obsession map onto the exact problem they're solving. It's the difference between "smart person picked a hot space" and "this person has been involuntarily thinking about this problem for a decade." Strong founder-market fit is the single most predictive early-stage signal.

**Reference Check (Backchannel)** — Diligence on a founder conducted through people *they did not introduce you to* — former colleagues, reports, customers, or co-founders. Because on-list references are curated to be positive, the backchannel is where you learn how someone behaves under stress, handles conflict, and treats people with no leverage over them.

## Real-World Example

Sequoia's early bet on Brian Chesky and Airbnb is the canonical case. On paper — air mattresses in strangers' apartments during a recession — the *idea* was easy to reject, and most investors did. What Paul Graham and later Sequoia underwrote was the *founders*: the relentlessness of physically flying to New York to meet hosts, reshoot listing photos, and learn the market door-to-door. That "do things that don't scale" velocity, plus a designer-founder's unusual obsession with the guest experience, was the founder-market-fit signal that the idea itself obscured. The lesson cuts both ways — Theranos shows how pedigree and charisma (a Stanford-dropout narrative, a board of statesmen) can substitute for substance when investors skip the backchannel and never reference-check the science or the people who'd left.

## Jamie's Action Prompt

Take the most recent founder NWA is seriously considering and write down two things before your next conversation: (1) the *specific, non-obvious* reason this person — not a generic smart founder — is unreasonably suited to this market (if you can't articulate it crisply, that's a finding); and (2) one backchannel reference you could reach who is *not* on the founder's list. Then actually make that call. The gap between the curated story and the backchannel is where your angel edge lives.
