An angel syndicate is a group of individual investors who pool capital to make a single investment, typically through a Special Purpose Vehicle (SPV) — a legal entity created solely to hold one deal. Instead of writing a $25K check solo, you join a group writing a $250K–$1M check together, usually led by an experienced "lead" angel who sourced the deal, negotiated terms, and manages the relationship with the founder.

For angel investors like you, syndicates solve two critical problems: access and diversification. A solo angel writing $25K checks often can't get into competitive rounds where minimums are $100K+. A syndicate aggregates enough capital to matter. More importantly, it lets you spread your annual angel allocation across 8–12 deals instead of 2–3, which is how you actually play the power law (more on that in a future lesson). The tradeoff is real, though: you're trusting the lead's judgment and deal flow, your information rights flow through an intermediary, and you typically pay 20% carry to the lead on any gains.

Here's what the textbooks won't tell you: **the quality of the lead is the entire bet.** A great lead has proprietary deal flow from founder networks and sector expertise — they see deals before they hit AngelList or SeedInvest. A weak lead aggregates deals that are already broadly syndicated, which means you're getting adverse selection. Today's news is proof: organized syndicates led by operators (like the Ramp CTO and PubMatic CEO) are now co-leading institutional rounds. That's the tier you want to be in — not the one repackaging leftovers.

## Key Terms

**SPV (Special Purpose Vehicle)** — A single-purpose legal entity (usually an LLC) created to make one investment. Investors buy units in the SPV; the SPV holds equity in the startup. This separates each deal cleanly for liability and tax purposes.

**Carry (Carried Interest)** — The lead's share of profits, typically 20%. If the SPV invested $500K and exits at $5M, the lead takes 20% of the $4.5M gain ($900K) and investors split the remaining $3.6M pro-rata. No carry if the deal loses money.

## Real-World Example

When Figma was raising its Series A in 2015, it wasn't yet obvious that a browser-based design tool would eat Adobe's lunch. Dylan Field couldn't easily fill the round from top-tier VCs alone. An angel syndicate organized on AngelList — led by an investor with design-tool expertise — wrote a meaningful check alongside Index Ventures. Those syndicate members saw a 1,000x+ paper return when Adobe offered $20B in 2022. The lesson: the syndicate lead's domain expertise in design tools was what identified the opportunity. No generalist would have caught it that early.
