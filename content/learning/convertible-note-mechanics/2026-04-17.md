A convertible note is a short-term debt instrument that converts into equity at a future priced round — typically a Series A. It's the workhorse of angel investing because it lets you put money to work without the time and legal cost of negotiating a full valuation. But the devil is in the details: three mechanics determine whether you get a great deal or a mediocre one.

**The discount** gives you a percentage off whatever price the next round's investors pay. A 20% discount means if Series A investors pay $10/share, you convert at $8. It's your reward for taking early risk. **The valuation cap** sets a ceiling on the price at which your note converts — if the startup's value skyrockets, you still convert at the capped price, giving you more shares. **The interest rate** (typically 4–8% annually) accrues on your principal and converts into additional equity at the trigger event. Most angels focus on the cap and discount and forget that interest can meaningfully increase your share count over 18–24 months.

Here's the coaching insight that experienced VCs know: *the cap matters more than the discount in almost every scenario.* In a hot market like today's — where AI seed valuations are being bid up — a note without a cap is essentially a blank check on your dilution. Always insist on a cap. And watch for "MFN" (most favored nation) clauses: if the company issues notes to later investors on better terms, you automatically get those terms too. It's your insurance policy against being the early, naive money.

## Key Terms

**Valuation Cap** — The maximum effective valuation at which your convertible note converts to equity, regardless of how high the actual Series A valuation is. Lower cap = more equity for you.

**MFN (Most Favored Nation) Clause** — A provision that automatically upgrades your note terms to match any better terms offered to subsequent note investors before conversion.

## Real-World Example

When Airbnb raised its seed round in 2009, early investors like Sequoia used convertible notes with a $1.5M cap. By the time the Series A priced at a much higher valuation, those notes converted at the cap — giving seed investors ownership that was worth hundreds of millions at IPO. Without the cap, they'd have converted at the Series A price and earned a fraction of that return. The cap was the single most valuable term in that deal.
