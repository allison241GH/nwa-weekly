A normal covered call requires you to own 100 shares of the stock. On QQQ at ~$726, that's about **$72,600 of capital tied up** just to sell one call and collect maybe $900 in premium. The **poor man's covered call (PMCC)** is a way to run essentially the same income trade for a fraction of that cash. Instead of buying 100 shares, you buy one deep **in-the-money LEAPS call** — a long-dated call option (typically 9–24 months out) with a high delta, meaning it moves almost dollar-for-dollar with the stock. That long call *stands in* for the shares. Then, just like a regular covered call, you sell a shorter-dated out-of-the-money call against it and collect the premium. Mechanically it's a **diagonal spread**; in spirit it's "renting out upside on shares you don't fully own."

Why this matters for a covered-call seller: capital efficiency. A deep-ITM QQQ LEAPS might cost, say, $150 per share ($15,000 for one contract) versus $72,600 for the shares — so you control a QQQ-sized position for roughly a fifth of the cash, and the premium you collect from the short call is a *much larger percentage of what you actually put in*. That leverage cuts both ways, which is exactly the point a beginner needs to internalize: the PMCC amplifies your return on capital, but it also removes two things real shares give you — the **dividends** (the LEAPS holder doesn't receive them) and the indefinite holding period (your LEAPS expires, shares don't). It's a more capital-efficient trade *and* a more actively-managed one.

Here's the coaching insight beginners miss: **the PMCC's hidden enemy is the short call getting breached to the upside.** With real shares, if the stock rockets past your short strike, you just deliver shares you own and book the if-called return — annoying, but clean. With a PMCC, a sharp rally can push your short call's losses faster than your LEAPS gains for a stretch (because your LEAPS delta is high but not a full 1.00), and you can end up needing to roll the short call up-and-out to avoid the whole spread inverting on you. The rule of thumb: **buy the LEAPS deep enough (delta ~0.80+) that it behaves like stock, and sell the short call far enough OTM that you're not constantly defending it.** And never let the *total* cost of your LEAPS minus premiums collected exceed the width you could lose — know your max loss before you open it. The PMCC is a fantastic tool for a high-priced underlying like QQQ; it is *not* a free lunch, and it demands you actually manage the roll.

## Key Terms

**LEAPS** — "Long-term Equity AnticiPation Securities": simply options with expirations far in the future, typically 9 months to 2+ years out. In a PMCC you buy a *deep in-the-money* LEAPS call so it has a high delta and moves nearly one-for-one with the stock — serving as a cheaper substitute for owning 100 shares.

**Poor Man's Covered Call (PMCC)** — A diagonal call spread that mimics a covered call: buy one long-dated deep-ITM call (the stock stand-in), then sell shorter-dated OTM calls against it to collect premium. It requires far less capital than a true covered call but forgoes dividends and must be actively managed around the short strike.

## Worked Example

Take QQQ at ~$726 this week. A true covered call means owning 100 shares (~$72,600) and selling, say, the 45-DTE $750 call for ~$12.50 — collecting ~$1,250 against $72,600, roughly a 1.7% static yield. A PMCC version might instead buy a Jan-2028 QQQ $580 LEAPS call (deep ITM, ~0.85 delta) for perhaps ~$185/share, or ~$18,500 for the contract — then sell that *same* 45-DTE $750 call for ~$1,250. Now you've collected the same ~$1,250 premium against ~$18,500 of capital at risk instead of $72,600 — a far higher return *on the cash you committed*. The trade-offs are concrete: you skip QQQ's small September distribution (the LEAPS earns no dividend), and if QQQ blasts through $750 before Aug 28 you'll likely need to roll the short call up-and-out rather than simply delivering shares. Note this is exactly why QQQ — high share price, no near-term ex-div in the window — is a cleaner PMCC candidate this cycle than Ford, whose $14 shares make 100 of them cheap to own outright anyway and whose Aug 11 dividend you'd forfeit.
