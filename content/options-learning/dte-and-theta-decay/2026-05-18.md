When you sell a covered call, you're selling *time*. Every option has two pieces of value: **intrinsic value** (how far ITM it is — zero for the OTM calls you'll sell) and **time value** (everything else — the premium you collect for the *possibility* the stock moves your way before expiration). That time value bleeds away as expiration approaches, and the rate of that bleed is called **theta**. As the option's seller, **theta works for you**: every day that passes, the call you sold is worth a little less to buy back, which is profit accruing to you. **DTE** — days to expiration — is the dial that controls how this plays out.

Here's the non-obvious part: **time decay is not linear — it accelerates as expiration nears.** A 90-day option loses its time value slowly at first; a 30-day option decays much faster per day; in the final week, theta is brutal. That's why the **30–45 DTE window is the covered-call sweet spot.** You're far enough out to collect a meaningful premium, but close enough that decay is already steep and working hard in your favor. Selling 7–10 DTE calls captures the fastest decay but pays little per trade and chains you to constant management and assignment scares; selling 90+ DTE calls collects a big premium up front but the money comes in slowly and you've locked up your upside for a whole quarter.

The coaching insight: **annualize, don't anchor on the headline premium.** A 60-day call paying $16 *looks* better than a 30-day call paying $9 — but $9 collected twice in 60 days is $18, beating the single $16, *and* you got two chances to reset your strike as the stock moved. Shorter-dated calls usually win on annualized yield precisely because of accelerating theta. The catch is friction: more trades, more commissions, more decisions, and more ex-dividend dates to dodge. The 30–45 DTE window is the pragmatic balance between harvesting fast decay and not living inside your brokerage app.

## Key Terms

**DTE (days to expiration)** — The number of calendar days until the option expires. The main lever for how fast premium decays and how often you re-trade. Covered-call sweet spot: ~30–45 DTE.

**Theta** — The amount of value an option loses per day from time decay, all else equal. As the *seller*, theta accrues to you. Theta accelerates as expiration approaches.

**Time decay** — The erosion of an option's time value as expiration nears. Non-linear: slow when far out, steep in the final weeks. The engine of covered-call income.

## Worked Example

You compare two QQQ calls. The 30-DTE $760 call pays ~$9; the 60-DTE $775 call pays ~$16. The 60-day premium is bigger in absolute terms, but annualize it: $9 over 30 days ≈ $9 × 12 = ~$108/yr of premium pace, versus $16 over 60 days ≈ $16 × 6 = ~$96/yr. The shorter call wins on yield *and* lets you reset the strike monthly as QQQ drifts — though you'll pay two commissions and check it twice as often. That's the trade theta is asking you to make.

## Jamie's Action Prompt

Take one of your positions and write down the premium for a ~30 DTE and a ~60 DTE call at similar deltas. Annualize both (premium ÷ DTE × 365). Notice which actually pays more per year — it's usually the shorter one. Then ask whether the extra management and the closer ex-dividend dates are worth that edge for you. There's no universal right answer, but making the calculation explicit is what separates an income strategy from a guess.
