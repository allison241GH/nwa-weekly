So far this curriculum has been about the *right* side of a stock position — you already own shares and you sell covered calls against them for income. The **wheel** adds the *left* side: a way to get *paid* while you wait to buy a stock you'd be happy to own anyway. It starts with a **cash-secured put**. When you sell a put, you take a premium in exchange for the obligation to *buy* 100 shares at the strike price if the stock falls to it by expiration. "Cash-secured" simply means you set aside the full purchase amount (strike × 100) so you can honor that obligation — no margin, no leverage, no surprise. If the stock stays above your strike, the put expires worthless and you keep the premium as pure income. If it drops below, you buy the shares you wanted — at a discount to today's price, further reduced by the premium you already collected.

For a covered-call seller, this matters because it closes the loop into a repeatable cycle: **sell cash-secured puts on a stock you want → get assigned and buy the shares → sell covered calls on those shares → get assigned and sell the shares → start over.** That's the wheel. You are collecting premium at *every* stage — while waiting to buy, and while waiting to sell — turning a stock you're neutral-to-bullish on into a stream of income regardless of which way it chops. The two halves you've already learned (covered calls) and the new half (cash-secured puts) are mirror images: a covered call caps your upside for income, a cash-secured put commits your cash to a downside entry for income.

Here's the coaching insight a beginner misses: **only sell a cash-secured put at a strike where you would genuinely be thrilled to own the stock — and size it to cash you actually have.** The premium is seductive, but selling a put is not a bet that the stock goes up; it's a *standing offer to buy at a price you chose*. If you'd regret owning the shares at the strike, don't sell the put, no matter how fat the premium. The wheel works because it only ever puts you into stocks you already wanted at prices you already liked; it breaks the moment you sell puts on names you don't actually want, chasing yield — because then assignment hands you a bag you'll be tempted to panic-sell exactly when you should hold.

## Key Terms

**Cash-Secured Put** — Selling a put option while setting aside the full cash needed to buy the shares if assigned (strike × 100 per contract). You collect premium now; in exchange you're obligated to buy at the strike if the stock is below it at expiration. Your effective cost basis if assigned = strike − premium collected.

**The Wheel** — A repeating income cycle: sell cash-secured puts to (potentially) acquire a stock at a discount, then sell covered calls on the acquired shares to (potentially) exit at a profit, collecting premium at each step and restarting after the shares are called away.

## Worked Example

Say you'd be happy to own more Ford at $13 (it trades ~$13.36 today). You sell one 30-day cash-secured put at the **$13 strike** for, illustratively, ~$0.25 — collecting **$25** and setting aside **$1,300**. Two outcomes: (1) Ford stays above $13 → the put expires worthless, you keep the $25 (~1.9% on your set-aside cash in a month, roughly 23% annualized if repeated) and can sell another put. (2) Ford closes below $13 → you buy 100 shares at $13, but your *effective* cost is **$12.75** ($13 − $0.25). Now you own the shares and rotate to the *covered-call* half of the wheel — selling, say, a $14 call against them for more premium. Note the same event-awareness from this week's Watch applies to the put side too: selling a Ford put *across* the ~July 29 earnings date means you could be assigned right into a gap-down, so a beginner should keep the first wheel turns *before* earnings or size them small.

## Jamie's Action Prompt

Pick one name you'd genuinely want to own more of — QQQ or Ford — and write down the *exact* price at which you'd be glad to buy 100 shares (or the right multiple for QQQ's price). Then check what a ~30-day cash-secured put at that strike would pay, and confirm you have the set-aside cash. Don't place it yet — just prove to yourself the strike is one you'd welcome assignment at, *before* the premium tempts you.
