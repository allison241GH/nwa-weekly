Every covered call decision comes down to one choice: **which strike?** And to choose well, you need the language of **moneyness** — where the strike sits relative to today's share price. For a *call*, a strike **above** the current price is **out-of-the-money (OTM)**, a strike right *at* the price is **at-the-money (ATM)**, and a strike *below* the price is **in-the-money (ITM)**. Covered-call sellers almost always sell **OTM** calls. Why? An OTM strike means the stock has to *rise* before your shares are at risk of being called away — so you keep some upside, and you only get assigned if the trade goes well enough that selling at that price is a good outcome anyway.

The second tool is **delta**. Delta technically measures how much the option's price moves per $1 move in the stock, but for covered-call selling there's a more useful shortcut: **an option's delta is a rough estimate of the probability it finishes in-the-money.** A 0.30-delta call has roughly a 30% chance of being ITM at expiration — meaning about a 70% chance it expires worthless and you keep your shares. That's why a common covered-call target is the **0.20–0.35 delta** range: it's far enough OTM that you'll usually *not* be called away, while still collecting a worthwhile premium. Selling a 0.50-delta (ATM) call doubles your premium but makes assignment a coin flip; selling a 0.10-delta call is very safe but pays almost nothing.

The coaching insight: **don't pick a strike by the dollar amount of premium — pick it by delta, then read the premium as the reward for that risk.** A beginner sees "$13 premium, nice" and sells it without realizing it's a 0.45-delta call that's likely to get assigned. A disciplined seller decides "I want roughly a 30% assignment chance" *first*, finds the strike with ~0.30 delta, and *then* evaluates whether the premium it pays is worth it. Same goes across different stocks: a 0.30-delta call on volatile Ford sits much further OTM (in percentage terms) than a 0.30-delta call on calm QQQ, because Ford can travel farther.

## Key Terms

**Out-of-the-money (OTM)** — For a call, a strike *above* the current share price. The stock must rise past it before your shares are at risk. Covered-call sellers favor OTM strikes to preserve some upside.

**Delta** — How much the option price changes per $1 move in the underlying, and — handy shortcut — an approximation of the probability the option expires in-the-money. A 0.30-delta call ≈ 30% chance of assignment.

**Moneyness** — The relationship between strike and current price: OTM (call strike above price), ATM (at the price), ITM (below the price).

## Worked Example

QQQ trades at ~$744. A $760 call (about 2% OTM) might carry a ~0.30 delta — roughly a 30% chance of being called away, paying a steady premium. A $775 call (about 4% OTM) might be ~0.22 delta — safer for your shares, less premium. Now Ford at ~$16.15: a $17 call (about 5% OTM) can *also* be ~0.30 delta, because Ford's higher volatility means it can move 5% as easily as QQQ moves 2%. Same delta, very different percentage cushion — that's the volatility difference showing up in the strike.

## Jamie's Action Prompt

Pull up an option chain for QQQ and find the call strike closest to 0.30 delta in a ~30–45 day expiration. Note how far OTM it is in percentage terms. Do the same for Ford. Compare the two percentages — that gap *is* the market's read on which name is more volatile, and it's the single most useful intuition for sizing your strikes going forward.
