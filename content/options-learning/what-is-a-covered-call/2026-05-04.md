A **covered call** is the most conservative options strategy there is, and it's the one to learn first. The setup is simple: you already own at least **100 shares** of a stock or ETF, and you *sell* (write) one call option against those shares. In exchange, you collect a cash payment up front — the **premium** — that's yours to keep no matter what happens next. What you give up is the right to unlimited gains above a chosen price: if the stock closes above your **strike price** at expiration, your 100 shares get sold ("called away") at that strike. The word "covered" means you actually own the shares to deliver — which is what makes this far safer than selling a call you can't back.

For a long-term holder like you, the appeal is that a covered call turns a static share position into an *income-producing* one. You're essentially renting out your shares' upside for a month or two at a time and getting paid rent. On a position you'd be comfortable holding anyway — say your QQQ or Ford shares — that rent compounds into a meaningful yield over a year. The trade-off is real but bounded: in a runaway rally you'll cap your gains at the strike, and you keep all the downside if the stock falls (minus the small cushion the premium gives you). That's why covered calls fit best on shares you're neutral-to-mildly-bullish on, not the ones you expect to double.

The coaching insight that beginners miss: **the premium is income, but the strike you choose is a decision about how much upside you're willing to sell.** Pick a strike close to today's price and you collect more premium but get called away easily; pick one far out-of-the-money and you keep more upside but earn less. Everything else in this curriculum — moneyness, delta, days-to-expiration, ex-dividend timing — is just refining those two levers: how much income, and how much upside you're renting out.

## Key Terms

**Covered call** — Selling one call option for every 100 shares you own. "Covered" because you hold the underlying shares to deliver if assigned, so there's no unlimited-loss risk like a naked call.

**Premium** — The cash the buyer pays you for the call, credited to your account immediately and kept regardless of outcome. Quoted per share, so a $0.30 premium on one contract = $30 (100 shares).

**Strike price** — The price at which you've agreed to sell your shares if the option is exercised. Above it at expiration → you're called away; below it → the call expires worthless and you keep your shares and the premium.

## Worked Example

You own 100 shares of Ford (F) at ~$16.15. You sell one call with a $17.00 strike expiring in about a month for a $0.30 premium. You immediately collect **$30**. Two outcomes at expiration: (1) F is below $17 — the call expires worthless, you keep your shares *and* the $30, and you can sell another call next month. (2) F closes above $17 — your shares are sold at $17, for a gain of $0.85/share *plus* the $0.30 premium = $1.15/share (~7%), even though you gave up any gains above $17. Either way you got paid to wait.

## Jamie's Action Prompt

Look at your QQQ and Ford positions. For each, ask: "Would I be genuinely fine selling these shares 3–5% higher than today?" If yes, you're a candidate to write a covered call. If the answer is "no, I'd hate to lose these on a breakout," that tells you to either pick a much further-out strike or skip the trade — and that instinct is exactly the judgment covered-call selling trains.
