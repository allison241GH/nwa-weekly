It's worth being precise about what **assignment** actually is, because beginners often fear it like a loss when it's usually a *good* outcome. Assignment simply means the call you sold was exercised, so you sold your 100 shares at the strike. It only happens when the stock is *above* your strike — which means you made money: the appreciation from your cost up to the strike, plus the premium you kept. You weren't "stopped out" or "blown up"; you sold at a price you pre-agreed was acceptable, and got paid extra to do it. The only real cost of assignment is **opportunity cost** — the gains *above* the strike that you no longer get. That's the upside you rented out in exchange for the premium. If you genuinely didn't want to part with the shares, the lesson is to choose a higher strike or skip the call, not to dread the mechanic.

The flip side — the part Jamie cares about under "share protection" — is what a covered call does to your *downside*. The premium you collect is a **downside cushion**: it lowers your effective cost basis by exactly the premium received. If you own Ford at $16.15 and collect $0.30, you don't break even until the stock falls below $15.85; the first $0.30 of any decline is absorbed by income you already banked. It's important to be honest about the size of this protection: a covered call is a *partial, shallow* hedge, not real insurance. It cushions a small dip and softens a flat-to-down market, but in a serious drawdown the premium is a rounding error against the share losses — and worse, your upside is still capped if the rebound overshoots your strike. For actual downside *protection* you'd buy a put; a covered call only ever protects you by the width of the premium.

The coaching insight that ties this whole curriculum together: **a covered call is the right trade when you're neutral-to-mildly-bullish and want income with a little cushion — and the wrong trade when you're strongly bullish or trying to hedge a crash.** The dual goal of "income + share protection" is real but modest on the protection side. Size your expectations accordingly: write calls on shares you'd be content to sell a bit higher, treat the premium as both rent and a thin cushion, and accept assignment gracefully when it comes. Do that consistently and the strategy quietly adds a few percent of annualized yield to positions you were holding anyway — which, on a long-term core like QQQ and Ford, is exactly the point.

## Key Terms

**Assignment** — Your shares being sold at the strike because the call was exercised. Happens only when the stock is above your strike, so it's a *profitable* exit — the premium plus appreciation to the strike — not a loss.

**Downside cushion** — The amount the premium lowers your effective break-even. Collect $0.30 on a $16.15 stock and you're protected down to $15.85. Real but shallow — it softens small dips, not crashes.

**Opportunity cost** — The gains above your strike that you forgo when assigned. The true (and only) "cost" of a covered call, and the price you accept in exchange for the premium.

## Worked Example

Two endings for a Ford $17 call sold at $0.30 with shares at $16.15. **Assigned:** Ford finishes at $17.40; your shares sell at $17 (you keep the $0.85 gain to the strike + $0.30 premium = $1.15, ~7%), and you forgo only the $0.40 above $17 — opportunity cost, not a loss. **Cushion at work:** Ford instead drifts to $15.95; the call expires worthless, you keep the $0.30, and your $0.20 paper loss is fully absorbed by the premium — you're net *up*. The painful case is a *crash* to $13: the $0.30 barely dents a $3 share loss. That asymmetry is the honest shape of "share protection."

## Jamie's Action Prompt

For one of your positions, write the two sentences that should make you comfortable *before* selling any call: "If assigned at [strike], I'm happy because I made [if-called return]," and "If it dips, my real protection is only [premium] — I am not hedged against a serious drop." Saying both out loud calibrates your expectations correctly: income and a thin cushion, yes; a crash hedge, no. That clear-eyed framing is what makes covered-call selling a durable habit instead of a disappointment.
