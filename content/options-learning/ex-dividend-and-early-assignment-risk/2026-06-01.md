Most of the time, the call you sold can only be exercised against you at expiration — but that's not quite true. U.S. equity and ETF options are **American-style**, meaning the buyer can exercise *any* day before expiration. In practice they almost never do, because exercising early throws away the option's remaining time value. There's exactly one situation where it becomes rational, and every covered-call seller has to know it cold: **the day before an ex-dividend date, on an in-the-money call.** This is the single calendar risk that can surprise a beginner, and it's the reason the **ex-dividend date** belongs on your radar before you ever sell a call.

Here's the mechanism. To collect a stock's dividend, you must own the shares *before* the **ex-dividend date** (the day the stock starts trading without the upcoming dividend). The holder of a call doesn't get the dividend — but if they *exercise* the day before ex-div, they convert the call into shares in time to capture it. They'll do this when the dividend they'd capture is worth more than the time value they'd forfeit by exercising early — which happens when the call is **in-the-money** and close to expiration. If you're short that call, you get **assigned early**: your shares are called away the day before ex-div, you deliver them at the strike, and — the sting — *you* miss the dividend you were expecting as the shareholder. Your premium is still yours, but the trade ended on someone else's schedule.

The coaching insight, and the rule to internalize: **never let a covered call sit in-the-money going into an ex-dividend date you care about.** The fix is simple and entirely in your control. First, *know the ex-div dates* of everything you write calls on — they're scheduled quarterly for most names. Second, keep the strike comfortably OTM through any ex-div inside your option's life, so early exercise stays irrational. Third, if the stock rallies and your call goes ITM as ex-div approaches, **roll it up-and-out before the ex-div date** (buy it back, sell a higher/later one) to remove the assignment incentive — or simply accept assignment if the if-called return is fine with you. The danger is never the dividend itself; it's *not knowing the date* and getting called away by surprise.

## Key Terms

**Ex-dividend date** — The cutoff day for a dividend: you must own the shares *before* this date to receive the payout. On the ex-div date the stock typically opens lower by roughly the dividend amount.

**Early assignment** — Being forced to sell your shares before expiration because the call buyer exercised early. For covered calls, the main trigger is an ITM call the day before an ex-dividend date.

**American-style option** — An option exercisable any time up to expiration (U.S. equity/ETF options are American-style), which is what makes early assignment around ex-div possible. (European-style options, like many index options, can only be exercised at expiration.)

## Worked Example

You own Ford and sell a covered call that you later roll into a **September** expiration. Ford's ex-dividend date is ~**Aug 11** ($0.15). If the stock rallies and your call is ITM as Aug 11 nears, a call holder can exercise on ~Aug 10 to grab the $0.15 dividend — your shares vanish at the strike the day before ex-div, and you miss the payout. Compare that to writing a call that *expires in July*, cleanly before Aug 11: no ex-div inside its life, no early-assignment incentive, no surprise. Same stock, same strike logic — the *calendar* was the whole difference.

## Jamie's Action Prompt

Before your next covered call, look up the ex-dividend date of the underlying (your broker or any dividend calendar lists it). Ask: "Does an ex-div date fall inside this option's life, and would my strike be ITM near it?" If yes on both, either pick an expiration that ends *before* ex-div, move the strike further OTM, or plan to roll before the date. Make checking the ex-div calendar a fixed step in your pre-trade checklist — it's the one covered-call risk that's both easy to miss and easy to avoid.
