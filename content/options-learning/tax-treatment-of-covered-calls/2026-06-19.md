Here's the part of covered-call writing nobody mentions when they show you the premium: how it's taxed. When you sell a covered call and it **expires worthless** — the best, most common outcome — the premium you kept is a **short-term capital gain**, taxed at your ordinary income rate, no matter how long you've held the underlying shares. The holding period of your stock doesn't transfer to the option. So that "1.9% of premium" on QQQ or "2.4%" on Ford isn't 1.9% in your pocket; it's 1.9% *before* your marginal tax rate takes a bite, and at a high bracket that bite is meaningfully larger than the long-term rate you'd pay on the shares themselves.

Why this matters for a covered-call seller specifically: the strategy is an *income* strategy, and income is taxed less favorably than long-term gains. If a call gets **assigned**, the premium folds into your proceeds on the stock sale — so a call you've held shares against for years can still produce a clean long-term gain on the shares *plus* the premium, which is fine. The danger is the in-between case: if you write a call that's too deep in-the-money against a stock you've held just under a year, certain "non-qualified" calls can actually **suspend or even reset your holding period**, converting what would have been a long-term gain on the shares into a short-term one. That's a tax accident created entirely by strike selection — and it's invisible unless you know to look.

The coaching insight a beginner misses: **after-tax yield is the only yield that matters, and it changes which strike you should write.** A slightly further-OTM, "qualified covered call" that protects your shares' long-term holding period can be worth more after tax than a richer-premium call that taints it. Most retail sellers optimize the gross premium on the screen and never notice they've quietly converted a 15–20% long-term rate into a 32–37% short-term one on the underlying. The fix isn't complicated — it's just knowing the rule exists before you click sell, and (for anything beyond a simple account) keeping the income strategy in a tax-advantaged account where the short-term character of the premium stops mattering at all.

## Key Terms

**Short-Term Capital Gain** — Profit on an asset held one year or less, taxed at your ordinary income rate (higher than the long-term rate). Premium from a covered call that expires worthless is *always* a short-term gain, regardless of how long you've owned the underlying shares.

**Qualified Covered Call** — A call that meets IRS rules on strike (not too deep in-the-money) and time to expiration, so that writing it does *not* suspend or reset the holding period of your shares. Writing *non*-qualified calls can convert a would-be long-term gain on your stock into a short-term one.

## Worked Example

Take this week's Ford candidate: the $15.00 / 30-DTE call at ~$0.34 premium on 100 shares = **$34** of income. If it expires worthless, that $34 is a short-term gain — at a 35% marginal rate you keep ~$22, so the "real" static yield is closer to ~1.6% than the headline 2.4%. Now the trap: suppose you'd held those Ford shares for 11 months and, tempted by the rich IV, you wrote a *deep* in-the-money call instead of the OTM one above. That non-qualified call could suspend your holding period — so if you're later assigned or sell, the gain on the *shares* (potentially a much bigger number than $34) gets taxed as short-term too. You'd have traded a small premium edge for a large tax penalty on the position you actually care about.
