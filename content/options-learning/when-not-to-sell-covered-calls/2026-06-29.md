Most covered-call lessons teach you how to *sell* the call. This one teaches you when to keep your hands in your pockets — because the fastest way to turn a good income strategy into a regret machine is to sell calls mechanically, every week, regardless of what's happening. A covered call is a trade you make *against your own shares*: in exchange for premium today, you cap how much those shares can earn between now and expiration. That's a fine bargain when you expect the stock to drift sideways or up modestly. It's a *bad* bargain in three specific situations, and recognizing them is what separates a thoughtful seller from someone leaving real money on the table.

**First: don't sell calls into an event you can't predict.** The clearest example is earnings. Ford reports Q2 around July 29; a stock can gap 10% on an earnings surprise. If you've sold a call expiring after the report and the stock rockets, you're forced to hand over all the gains above your strike for a premium that never priced in that move properly. The market knows this, which is why premiums *look* fat before earnings — you're being paid more because the risk is genuinely higher. Unless you specifically want to be short that move, let earnings pass and write the call afterward. **Second: don't sell calls right after a sharp drop if you believe in the rebound.** QQQ just fell ~5% from its record. Selling a call now locks in a strike not far above today's depressed price — so if the bounce you're hoping for actually arrives, your shares get called away right as they recover, and you've capped the very rally that was the whole reason you held.

**Third: don't sell calls on shares you're not actually willing to lose.** A covered call is a standing offer to sell your stock at the strike. If a position is a long-term core holding you'd be upset to see assigned away — for tax reasons, conviction, or cost basis — then writing calls against it is quietly betting against your own thesis for a small premium. The coaching insight beginners miss: **the premium is never "free money."** It's payment for giving up the right tail of your returns. In a flat market that right tail is worth little, so selling is smart. Before an earnings gap, after a washout you expect to reverse, or on a stock you'd hate to part with, that right tail is worth a *lot* — and the premium rarely compensates you fairly for it.

## Key Terms

**Opportunity Cost (Capped Upside)** — The gains you forfeit when a stock rises above your strike. You still keep everything up to the strike plus the premium, but every dollar above the strike goes to the call buyer. It's invisible (you don't see it on a statement), which is exactly why it's the most underweighted cost in covered-call writing.

**Event Risk** — The chance of a large, scheduled-or-unscheduled price move from a known catalyst — earnings, a Fed decision, a product announcement, a dividend declaration. Options premiums rise ahead of known events because the market is pricing in the bigger potential move; selling into that elevated premium means you're being paid more *because* you're taking more risk.

## Worked Example

Say Ford is ~$14.13 today and you're tempted by a ~$15 call expiring Aug 14 paying ~$0.45 — a ~3.2% static yield that looks great. But Ford reports earnings ~July 29, *inside* that window. If Ford beats and jumps to $16, your shares get called at $15: you collect the $0.45 premium plus $0.87 of stock gain (to $15) = $1.32, but you forfeit the dollar from $15 to $16. You earned ~9% and *missed* ~7% more — and you took full earnings-gap risk to do it. Now compare a ~July 18 call that expires *before* earnings: smaller premium, but no event risk and your shares stay free to capture the report. Same with QQQ after its 5% drop — if you think the rotation reverses, a closer-dated, slightly-further-OTM strike (or simply *not* writing the full position) keeps the rebound yours instead of the call buyer's.

## Jamie's Action Prompt

Before you write a single call this week, run each candidate through a three-question filter: (1) Is there an earnings report or other catalyst *inside* this expiry? (2) Am I selling into a post-drop bounce I actually expect to happen? (3) Would I be genuinely fine if these exact shares got called away at this strike? If any answer gives you pause — like writing Ford across its ~July 29 earnings — either move the expiry *before* the event (~July 18 clears it) or skip that leg entirely. The discipline to *not* sell is itself a position.
