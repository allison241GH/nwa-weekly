Once you can pick a strike and a DTE, the next skill is *measuring* the trade so you can compare it to anything else you could do with the money. Covered-call sellers track two return numbers. The **static return** (sometimes "return if unchanged") asks: if the stock goes *nowhere* and the call expires worthless, what did the premium earn me? It's simply **premium ÷ stock price**. The **if-called return** asks: if the stock rises through my strike and I'm assigned, what's my *total* gain — the premium *plus* the appreciation from my cost to the strike? It's **(strike − price + premium) ÷ price**. Static is your "rent collected" case; if-called is your "happily sold at a profit" case. A well-chosen covered call should leave you content with *both* outcomes.

The number that lets you compare across different DTEs and stocks is the **annualized yield**: take the period return and scale it to a year — `return × (365 ÷ DTE)`. This is essential because raw premiums lie. A $0.30 premium on Ford and a $13 premium on QQQ are incomparable until you express them as yields against very different share prices and time frames. Annualizing reveals, for instance, that Ford's modest-looking $0.30 on a $16 stock over 30 days (~1.9% static → ~23% annualized) is actually a *higher* income rate than QQQ's chunky $13 on a $744 stock over 45 days (~1.7% static → ~14% annualized). The dollar figures hide that; the yield exposes it.

The coaching insight that keeps beginners out of trouble: **never chase annualized yield without looking at why it's high.** A fat annualized yield almost always means one of three things — high implied volatility (the stock is genuinely risky), a near-the-money strike (high assignment odds), or a very short DTE (lots of churn and friction). Ford pays more than QQQ because it *is* more volatile, not because it's a free lunch. Use annualized yield to *compare* trades of similar risk, not to hunt for the biggest number on the screen. The right covered call is the one whose static return you'd happily accept and whose if-called return you'd happily take — at a delta and DTE you can live with.

## Key Terms

**Static return** — Premium ÷ stock price. What you earn if the stock is unchanged and the call expires worthless. Your baseline "rent."

**If-called return** — (Strike − stock price + premium) ÷ stock price. Your total gain if the stock rises through the strike and you're assigned: premium *plus* appreciation up to the strike.

**Annualized yield** — A period return scaled to a full year: return × (365 ÷ DTE). The only fair way to compare premiums across different stocks, strikes, and expirations.

## Worked Example

Ford at $16.15, sell the $17 / 30-DTE call for $0.30. Static = 0.30 ÷ 16.15 = **1.9%**; annualized ≈ 1.9% × (365÷30) ≈ **23%**. If-called = (17 − 16.15 + 0.30) ÷ 16.15 = 1.15 ÷ 16.15 = **7.1%** in a month. Now QQQ at $744, sell the $765 / 45-DTE call for $13. Static = 13 ÷ 744 = **1.7%**; annualized ≈ 1.7% × (365÷45) ≈ **14%**. If-called = (765 − 744 + 13) ÷ 744 = **4.6%**. Ford's annualized static yield is higher — but that extra ~9 points is volatility risk, not magic.

## Jamie's Action Prompt

For the next covered call you're considering, write down all three numbers before you place it: static return, if-called return, and annualized yield. Then say out loud, "I'm fine if this expires worthless and I keep [static], and I'm fine if I'm called away for [if-called]." If you can't honestly say both, change the strike or the DTE until you can. That two-sided comfort test is the whole discipline.
